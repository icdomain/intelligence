The single greatest factor determining AI performance is the volume and quality of training data. China, as a surveillance society, has already accumulated personal and biometric information on its citizens at massive scale, and faces little institutional resistance to channeling such data into social implementation and governance. The United States, grounded in individualism, operates under severe constraints on the collection, integration, and operational use of available data. This is the structural disadvantage of the United States in the AI development race. Moreover, at a moment when the possibility that AI is approaching general intelligence is widely felt, for the United States to apply the brakes on AI development while carrying this disadvantage would amount to voluntarily relinquishing its position as the hegemonic power. The AI revolution should therefore be understood as situated in a context that lends itself to a brakeless development race.

## Related Reading

The following works contain arguments that overlap with the line of reasoning developed here.

- Kai-Fu Lee, *AI Superpowers: China, Silicon Valley, and the New World Order* (Houghton Mifflin Harcourt, 2018) — A structural analysis of the U.S.-China AI competition from inside the industry, framing it as an asymmetry of data volume, implementation environments, and state involvement. Provides a concrete breakdown of China's structural advantages.
- Paul Scharre, *Four Battlegrounds: Power in the Age of Artificial Intelligence* (W. W. Norton, 2023) — A strategic study that decomposes the AI development race into four domains—data, computing resources, talent, and institutions—and systematically analyzes the structural asymmetries between the United States and China.
- Shoshana Zuboff, *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism: The Fight for a Human Future at the New Frontier of Power* (PublicAffairs, 2019) — A comprehensive study that positions data extraction at the core of a new economic order and argues for the inseparability of surveillance and technological development.
- Graham Allison, *Destined for War: Can America and China Escape Thucydides's Trap?* (Houghton Mifflin Harcourt, 2017) — A strategic study that typologizes structural conflicts between established hegemons and rising powers from historical cases and applies the framework to contemporary U.S.-China relations.
