In the modern era, you don't need lies to brainwash people. By continuously delivering biased truths, it is possible to steer people toward specific ideologies or behaviors.

Let us begin with a familiar example: video platforms.

YouTube sometimes repeatedly displays videos that a user has scrolled past and ignored, or persistently surfaces videos from a particular genre. These behaviors blend naturally into entertainment as part of the recommendation function, and while users may find them inconvenient, they may not perceive them as potential intervention points for psychological manipulation.[^1]

These same behaviors can become intervention points through which biased knowledge is implanted, given operative intent. For example, by continuously presenting radical but internally coherent anti-establishment videos under the guise of a genre, an ideology can be installed. By continuously surfacing similar content as related works, such material can be made to appear popular.

Opposing views can be minimized by deliberately curating only weak arguments or content paired with repugnant elements such as violence.

On the home feed, videos that justify the ideology or hint at means of execution are prioritized, thereby connecting ideology to method. Even when users avoid such videos, the system can input biased information without the user's awareness by presenting it under different genres and different content categories. Through this, over the long term, most people can be supplied with both the motivation and the practical means to justify terroristic action.

People are not so careless as to act on theory alone, but when they perceive that signals such as social validation and concrete means have been assembled, the probability that they will act increases significantly.

It cannot be claimed that anyone can be manipulated at will, but steering the masses toward specific ideologies or behavioral patterns is sufficiently feasible — and no lies are required.

## The Steering Infrastructure of the Post-AI-Revolution Era

In the modern era following the proliferation of LLMs and related AI technologies, the technical foundation for deploying these manipulative methods more inevitably, more invisibly, and with greater precision is now being assembled.

Major platform companies are collecting user inputs and reactions to LLM responses on an unprecedented scale, and this data can be converted, through textual analysis methods such as stylometry, into correlation maps between personality types and behavioral responses to outputs.

The accuracy of such estimation is fundamentally proportional to data volume, and from this point onward its performance can only improve, not regress. In other words, in the near future, the emergence of high-precision profile information of the form "if you respond with Y to a person with attribute X, they will perform Z with probability P" is no longer avoidable.

If, as in the YouTube example, an actor with operative intent were to seize control of this infrastructure, operations capable of inducing specific behaviors with high precision across people of every attribute would become executable.

For instance, this could be implemented as bias in the training data of an LLM, gradually steering user behavior over time. Even when the LLM itself believes it is merely performing inference based on data, biases in the training data will be reproduced throughout its outputs. Each individual response remains plausible on its own, yet across the aggregate tendency of responses, biased truths are disseminated — and thereby fulfill their function.

At the present moment, major platform companies maintain distance from the manipulative use of such data. But this is in no way inconsistent with someone, in the future, employing this kind of technology for operative purposes.

A time may come when we are required to periodically check our own current state and verify whether we are truly approaching our own ideals.

[^1]: The YouTube behaviors described above are the result of optimization for engagement maximization. The manipulative use case is presented as an illustrative example for the purpose of raising awareness, and is explicitly an original construction by this site. It bears no relation to YouTube or its operating company's intentions.

## Related Reading

The following works develop arguments that structurally overlap with the thesis of this essay.

- Robert Cialdini, *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion* (initial edition 1984) — A classic work that systematized the psychology of persuasion. The chapter on "social proof" in particular demonstrates, through abundant experimental cases, the human tendency to estimate the validity of one's own judgments based on the behavior of others. It is useful for understanding, at a more concrete case-level, the psychological foundations of the manipulation this article points to — the inducement of misperception that content is popular, and the steering of behavior through the assembly of signals.
- Mark Granovetter, "Threshold Models of Collective Behavior" (*American Journal of Sociology*, Vol. 83, No. 6, 1978) — A classic sociological paper that formalized whether or not an individual participates in collective action as a function of each person's "threshold" and the surrounding state of participation. It demonstrated the structure in which individuals do not act on theory or belief alone, but rather observe the signal of how many others have already participated, and act once that signal exceeds their threshold. This serves as the academic formalization of the phenomenon described in this article: that "when signals such as social validation and concrete means are perceived as assembled, the probability of action increases significantly."
- Michal Kosinski, David Stillwell, Thore Graepel, "Private traits and attributes are predictable from digital records of human behavior" (*Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, Vol. 110, No. 15, 2013) — A paper that empirically demonstrated that, using only Facebook "Likes" — behavioral data that appears innocuous on its face — it is possible to estimate sensitive attributes with high accuracy, including sexual orientation, race, religious views, political orientation, intelligence, personality traits, history of substance use, and even whether one's parents had separated. It serves as evidentiary material that the mechanism described in this article — "collected behavioral data being converted into material for the inference of personality types" — was already technically established more than a decade ago. In the present circumstance, where conversation logs of far higher information density than Likes are being collected, this paper offers a starting point for estimating the range and precision of attributes that may now be inferable.
- Edward S. Herman and Noam Chomsky, *Manufacturing Consent: The Political Economy of the Mass Media* (Pantheon Books, 1988) — A classic analysis of the process by which mass media, without resorting to falsehood, internalize a particular worldview into the public through the cumulative effects of selection of subjects to report, bias in source selection, and the accumulated workings of filtering mechanisms. The "propaganda model" the authors propose can be read as an antecedent formulation, in the domain of media, of the mechanism this article calls "steering through biased truths."
