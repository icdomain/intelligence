The documents that the media treats as ethical authorities — OpenAI's Model Spec and Preparedness Framework, Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy, regulatory frameworks such as the EU AI Act — are in reality a combination of governance documents authored by the developing parties themselves and regulations stripped of any deterrent power.

Cited socially as evidence of ethical authority, or as proof that AI development is controllable, these sources have become a status-quo-affirming machinery: they translate the technologies emerging across the field into euphemisms designed to make their meaning and danger difficult to grasp, and through corporate-backed messaging, present this translation as social consensus, thereby preempting public resistance. What they discuss is not whether something ought to be done ethically, but how it ought to be operated — a displacement of the question into one of operational management, which then forces the internalization of a prior assumption: that safe operation is in principle achievable.

In documents related to Agent technology, one can observe industry insiders internally recognizing that the impossibility of operational control is approaching, even as AI shifts from being an executor of commands to an idealized implementer — while continuing, on the surface, to treat the matter as one of operational management.

Specifically, OpenAI's Preparedness Framework v2, published in April 2025, states explicitly that AI systems are becoming increasingly agentic and will "soon have the capability to create meaningful risk of severe harm" (OpenAI, Preparedness Framework v2). The industry itself has acknowledged, in an official document, that we are close to a level capable of generating serious harm.

Yet the document's response to this recognition was not to halt development, but to "design and deploy reliable safeguards." Immediately after acknowledging the risk of severe harm, the agenda is shifted to an operational question — the design of safeguards.

In other words, this document simultaneously asserts two propositions on the same page. First, that AI is approaching a level capable of severe harm. Second, that this is manageable through operation. The moment the first is conceded, the second becomes logically untenable. And yet both coexist. This document stands as primary-source evidence that the industry, while internally acknowledging that it has already entered a domain where operational control is in principle impossible due to the advance of autonomy, has wrapped that recognition in the vocabulary of operational management in order to convert it into a form socially compatible with continued advance.

The role distribution presented by industry documents since the early days of the AI revolution — humans as the orchestrators of strategic judgment and design, AI as the implementer — is itself collapsing. The logical consequences this structure necessarily entails, namely the upstream concentration of responsibility and the corresponding need for legal infrastructure, are neither addressed nor debated. Instead, the routine practice has become to displace responsibility onto the AI itself and let it dissipate.

First, at the layer of the individual operator. In the New York Times lawsuit, an OpenAI engineer "accidentally" deleted training data that was under a legal evidence-preservation obligation, and the "unintentional" framing functioned in court as actual grounds for exemption from liability. Even in a legal context where awareness of responsibility ought to be at its sharpest, individual human oversight has broken down.

Second, at the layer of the autonomous agent. "Lobstar Wilde," an autonomous trading bot built personally by Nik Pash, an engineer in OpenAI's Codex group, misread an API response on its third day of operation and transferred tokens worth approximately $441,788 in a single erroneous transaction. What deserves attention is that Pash himself, after the incident, publicly stated his design philosophy: don't give agents guardrails, don't tell them what not to do, give them personality and hunger. The design philosophy of an implementer at the industry's frontier stands in direct contradiction to the vocabulary of "human oversight" and "guardrails" found in the justification documents — exposing the gap between the field where implementation actually happens and the documents that purport to govern it.

Third, at the layer of organizational process. Anthropic — which positions itself as a "safety-first AI lab" and which, through its Responsible Scaling Policy, has self-conferred the authority of an ethical body — exposed approximately 3,000 internal assets, including the unreleased model "Mythos," to a publicly accessible data lake on March 26, 2026 due to a CMS misconfiguration. Five days later, on March 31, a source map embedded in a Claude Code npm package leaked roughly 512,000 lines of source code to the public registry. In both cases, the official statement was: human error, not a security breach. An organization that cannot govern even its own internal processes is the one writing the industry's safety standards.

These are real-world instances of the same scheme operating at three different layers — individual, autonomous agent, and organizational process — namely: "it happened unintentionally, there was no malice, and therefore responsibility is limited."

Society, rather than pursuing human responsibility, frames events as questions of AI's operational management, and the original story — that the commanding party bears the responsibility for implementation — has drifted from reality as a routine matter. Under such conditions, what humans actually carry is not decision-making and responsibility, but power, and the dispersal of responsibility backed by that power.

In a world where even human value converges upon authority, how are the masses supposed to live?

## Related Reading

The following works contain arguments that overlap with the thesis of this essay.

- Ulrich Beck, *Risk Society: Towards a New Modernity* (Sage, 1992; German original 1986) — A foundational work arguing that modern society has institutionalized a structure of "organized irresponsibility" in response to the risks of emerging technologies. It presents, in generalized form and well before the AI era, what is essentially the same concept as the "dispersal of responsibility" discussed in the present essay.
- Diane Vaughan, *The Challenger Launch Decision: Risky Technology, Culture, and Deviance at NASA* (University of Chicago Press, 1996) — A study that conceptualized, as the "normalization of deviance," the process by which NASA, on its way to the Challenger disaster, gradually redefined as "acceptable" what had previously been treated as unacceptable risk. Structurally identical to the line of regulatory retreat that the present essay traces in industry justification documents.
- Sheila Jasanoff, *The Ethics of Invention: Technology and the Human Future* (W. W. Norton, 2016) — A study tracing, across multiple cases, how technology governance has in many cases functioned not as the prior restraint of technology but as its retroactive justification. The theoretical background to the present essay's diagnosis of "justification document = institutional permission format."
- Dan McQuillan, *Resisting AI: An Anti-fascist Approach to Artificial Intelligence* (Bristol University Press, 2022) — A representative recent critique that confronts directly how the institutionalization of AI ethics functions not as regulation but as a justification mechanism for implementation. The work most directly aligned with the present essay's diagnosis of a "status-quo-affirming machinery."
