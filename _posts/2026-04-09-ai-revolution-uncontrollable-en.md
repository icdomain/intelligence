The AI revolution is already uncontrollable.

No realistic means exists to halt the development and social implementation of AI-related technologies on ethical grounds. This is the provisional assessment of this article. The assessment rests on the following two observations.

**Legitimization of social implementation through vocabulary manipulation.** The governance documents of major AI vendors have ceased to function as brake mechanisms for dangerous technology and have instead become apparatuses for legitimizing social implementation. The essence of agent technology — the functional reproduction of willpower and a technology for the displacement of human labor — is concealed through euphemisms disguised as technical terminology, and the published texts obscure facts and causation within the bounds of what the public will tolerate. As a result, the debates and legislative processes that should have arisen as society's normal response have been bypassed. For details, see "[AI Vendors' Governance Documents Are Not Brake Mechanisms but Social Implementation Legitimization Apparatuses]({{ site.baseurl }}/en/archives/2026/04/21/governance-as-legitimization/)."

**Retreat of safety standards and disappearance of intervention pathways.** The safety standards of major AI vendors have been in retreat since April 15, 2025. Halt provisions have been downgraded to industry-wide recommendations, risk tracking of autonomous behavior has been relaxed, and the survival of internal pathways through which organizational bodies might intervene in AI development on ethical grounds cannot be confirmed. For details, see "[Tracking Governance Documents of Major AI Vendors: 2023-2026]({{ site.baseurl }}/en/archives/2026/04/10/governance-documents-tracking/)."

Even for technologies that should be restrained on ethical grounds, the legitimization apparatus makes public mobilization difficult, and internal intervention pathways cannot be confirmed to survive. As long as these two conditions hold simultaneously, the assessment stands: no realistic means exists to halt the development and social implementation of AI-related technologies.

## Related Reading

The following works develop arguments that structurally overlap with the thesis of this essay.

- Ulrich Beck, *Risk Society: Towards a New Modernity* (Sage Publications, 1992) — In advanced technological societies, the producers of risk also seize control over the definition of risk — a structure Beck termed "organized irresponsibility." The fact that AI vendors define their own risk categories and exclude labor displacement can be read as a direct instance of this concept.
- David Collingridge, *The Social Control of Technology* (Frances Pinter, 1980) — A classic that formalized the dilemma of the social control of technology. At the early stage when technology is controllable, there is insufficient information to know its impact; by the stage when the impact becomes clear, the technology is already embedded in society and cannot be controlled. This article's assessment that "no realistic means exists to halt it" can also be situated as a consequence of this dilemma.
