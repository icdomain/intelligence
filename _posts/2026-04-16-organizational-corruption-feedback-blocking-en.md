Organizational corruption generally arises from the blockage of feedback.

Complaints, the voices of frontline workers, statistical data. When necessary feedback fails to reach the organization, it becomes unable to accurately grasp the consequences of its own actions. Decisions rendered on the basis of biased information cease to be optimal behavior for the organization as a whole, and are instead replaced by optimal behavior within the local vicinity centered on the decision-making body.

As these local decisions accumulate, coordination between departments breaks down, and the organization loses its function as a whole.

The public tends to focus on conspicuous misconduct such as embezzlement or corruption, but these are terminal symptoms of decay, not its cause. The cause lies in the blockage and distortion of feedback.

## Why Is Feedback Blocked in the First Place?

Because individuals skilled at individual optimization tend to be concentrated in the upper layers of organizations.

Individual optimization in human beings, so long as it is grounded in resource acquisition, converges toward the maximal pursuit of self-interest without incurring social exclusion. And when viewed against the structure of the legal system and cognitive biases, this is implemented as a behavioral pattern in which the individual pursues self-interest to the fullest while distancing themselves from inconvenient information, securing immunity through the framing of "I didn't know" or "I don't understand." This article designates this behavioral pattern the "didn't-know tactic."

The legal systems of humankind carry a structural vulnerability: those who recognized a situation but failed to do what they should have are held liable, whereas those who did not recognize the situation itself are exempted. This design is a necessary boundary line to prevent unjust punishment, but at the same time it generates an enormous incentive for the "didn't-know tactic."

In other words, blocking inconvenient feedback that would undermine the legitimacy of one's self-interest, and arranging an environment in which the situation cannot be recognized, itself functions as a form of immunity.

Furthermore, the "didn't-know tactic" possesses a sophistical property: it shifts onto the party attempting to expose it the entire cost of reconstructing the situation at the time, delineating the actual scope of authority, and verifying the alternatives that were feasible from within that authority. In practice, such verification is often impossible, and this is precisely why legal systems have remained vulnerable to the "didn't-know tactic."

In this way, the "didn't-know tactic" establishes itself as a strategy powerfully protected on both the legal and practical fronts.

## Selecting for the Capacity to Evade Responsibility

This behavioral pattern is clearly advantageous in a competitive society.

Suppose two individuals of equal ability: one recognizes problems and attempts to address them; the other arranges a system in which problems are not recognized. The former continues to pay the cost of addressing issues, but even when this constitutes social justice it does not convert into personal profit. The latter, by distorting feedback, can reduce their response cost to zero and maximize the time available for pursuing gain. On the surface, no complaints arise and no problems are visible, and this individual in fact generates substantial profit. When the outcomes are compared, which of the two will be more highly evaluated is self-evident.

This selection is not carried out intentionally. It is simply that, as long as evaluation systems can only measure surface numbers, those who internalize the cost of response are made to lose, while those who externalize it are made to gain. Organizational promotion mechanisms believe they are selecting for competence, but what they are in fact selecting for is the capacity to evade responsibility.

The larger the organization, the more stages this selection passes through. Each time the same selection is repeated at each stage, the proportion of individuals skilled in the "didn't-know tactic" increases. That is, the more an organization is composed of individuals excelling at individual optimization, the more it is subjected to pressure to block even the feedback it most needs.

## What Happens Once This Structure Has Taken Hold

The upper layers cease to want to know, and the front lines cease to want to tell. When frontline workers report a problem and find that no response follows, and this pattern repeats, they eventually give up on reporting altogether. The cost of reporting is borne by the front line, yet there is no guarantee of improvement. On the contrary, they may be recorded as "someone who is aware of the problem" and then saddled with the responsibility of addressing it. Judged rationally, not reporting is more advantageous to the individual.

And thus, as the result of each party acting in accordance with their own interest, feedback is blocked.

## Related Reading

The following works develop arguments that structurally overlap with the thesis of this essay.

- Robbins, Ira P., "The Ostrich Instruction: Deliberate Ignorance as a Criminal Mens Rea," *Journal of Criminal Law and Criminology*, Vol. 81, No. 2 (1990) — A historical analysis of the accumulated cases through which the doctrine of willful blindness developed. It can be referenced as a record of the fact that immunity through "I didn't know" has been repeatedly exploited.
- *Global-Tech Appliances, Inc. v. SEB S.A.*, 563 U.S. 754 (2011) — The U.S. Supreme Court decision that established the modern standard of the "willful blindness doctrine" in American law. The decision articulated the two-part test — that a defendant must "subjectively believe that there is a high probability that a fact exists" and must "take deliberate actions to avoid learning of that fact" — and applied it to a case of patent infringement. Even so, this does not resolve the immense cost of verification, and unconscious blindness is explicitly outside its scope. The vulnerability of the law remains intact, and the incentive for the "didn't-know tactic" remains enormous.
